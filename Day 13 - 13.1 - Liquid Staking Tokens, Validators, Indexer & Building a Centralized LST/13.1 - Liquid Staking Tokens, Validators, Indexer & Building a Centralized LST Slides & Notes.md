## 1. What is Staking?

**Staking** is a way to participate in the security and operation of a blockchain network while earning rewards. It involves locking up your cryptocurrency in a wallet or staking platform to support the network, validate transactions, and secure the blockchain.

### How Does Staking Work?

1. **Proof of Stake (PoS) Blockchains**:
   - Many blockchains, like Ethereum 2.0, Solana, and Cardano, use a system called **Proof of Stake (PoS)** instead of energy-intensive mining
   - In PoS, validators are chosen to create new blocks and verify transactions based on the amount of cryptocurrency they stake (lock up)

2. **Locking Your Tokens**:
   - When you stake your tokens, you are "locking" them in the blockchain
   - This shows your commitment to the network and qualifies you to earn rewards

3. **Earning Rewards**:
   - In return for staking, you earn rewards (like interest), typically in the form of the same cryptocurrency you staked

### Real-Life Example

Think of staking like **putting money in a fixed deposit at a bank**:
- You deposit your money (cryptocurrency) for a set period
- The bank (blockchain network) uses it for its operations
- In return, you earn interest (staking rewards)

### Key Benefits of Staking

1. **Earn Passive Income**: You earn rewards just for holding and staking your tokens
2. **Support the Network**: By staking, you help the blockchain stay secure and functional
3. **Eco-Friendly**: PoS and staking use far less energy compared to traditional mining

### Liquid Staking (Brief Introduction)

**Liquid staking** solves the lockup problem by issuing you a **liquid staking token** (e.g., stETH or mSOL), allowing you to use your staked funds in DeFi while still earning staking rewards.

> Staking is a process where you lock your cryptocurrency to help secure a blockchain network, validate transactions, and earn rewards. It's a passive way to grow your crypto while supporting the blockchain ecosystem.

## 2. Current Stake Pools and Validators

### What's Happening Here?

**Helius Validator** is listed as the **biggest validator on Solana**, managing **12,853,102 SOL** (worth about **$2 billion**).

However, **Helius does not own this SOL**. This large amount of SOL comes from **delegators** (users) who trust and stake their SOL with Helius.

```
         100 SOL    200 SOL    250 SOL    25000 SOL
User A ─────────────────────────────────────────────► Helius Validator
User B ───────────────────────────────►                (manages 12.8M SOL total)
User C ──────────────────────►
```

### Why Don't Validators Own This SOL?

- Validators like Helius are **service providers**, running the infrastructure needed to secure and validate transactions on the Solana blockchain
- Users (delegators) **delegate their SOL** to validators to help secure the network and earn rewards

### Why Would Delegators Stake with Helius?

1. **Reputation and Efficiency**: Helius is trusted for its reliability and low skip rates (missed blocks), making it a preferred choice
2. **Rewards Sharing**: Validators share the staking rewards with delegators after deducting a small commission (e.g., 3.3% for Helius)
3. **Decentralization Incentive**: Delegators help maintain the network's decentralization by staking across multiple validators

### Key Takeaways

- **Validators don't need to own SOL** — they act as trusted operators for staking
- The staked SOL comes from **delegators** who trust validators to secure the network
- **Earning Model**: Validators earn commissions on staking rewards, not from owning the staked tokens

> **Real-Life Analogy**: Think of validators as **fund managers** — they manage staked SOL on behalf of delegators while taking a small fee for their service.

### Key Insight About Helius

Helius is a unique validator on the Solana network because:

1. **No Commission Model**:
   - Helius does **not keep any portion of the staking rewards** for themselves
   - All rewards or interest generated by staking are returned to the delegators (users who staked their SOL with Helius)

2. **Most Validators Take a Commission**:
   - Other validators typically charge a **commission fee** (e.g., 3.3%, 5%, or even higher) on the rewards before passing the rest to the delegators
   - This fee is how validators usually fund their operations and infrastructure

## 3. Why Do I Not Get Equal Amount of HSOL for SOL?

Imagine you're trading **regular data storage (SOL)** for **cloud storage subscriptions (HSOL/mSOL)**. Here's how it works:

- **Regular Data Storage (SOL)**: This is like buying a regular USB drive. It works as it is, but it doesn't grow or give you extra benefits.

- **Cloud Storage Subscription (HSOL or mSOL)**: When you switch to cloud storage (HSOL/mSOL), you're not just paying for storage space. You're also getting **extra features** like automatic backups, syncing, and additional storage over time (these are like the **staking rewards** baked into HSOL/mSOL).

Because the cloud storage includes **more features and value**, 1 SOL won't give you 1 full unit of HSOL or mSOL. You'll get **less HSOL** or mSOL because their value per unit is **higher**.

### Technology Analogy in the Image

- **HSOL**: 1 SOL gives you **0.96 HSOL** — like upgrading to a basic cloud subscription with a few features
- **mSOL**: 1 SOL gives you **0.81 mSOL** — like upgrading to a premium cloud subscription with even more features baked in

### Why the Difference?

Different providers (**Helius** vs. **Marinade**) offer different benefits, which affects how much you get:
- **HSOL**: A standard cloud subscription with fewer accumulated features (staking rewards)
- **mSOL**: A premium subscription with more features and value built over time

### Key Takeaway

Switching SOL for HSOL or mSOL is like upgrading from basic data storage to **cloud storage with extra features**. You get **less quantity**, but the overall value you're receiving stays the same (or even grows).

## 4. Liquid Staking Token (LST)

An **LST** is like a **receipt** for staking your cryptocurrency.

- When you stake your crypto (lock it up to help secure the blockchain), you get an LST that **represents your staked assets**
- This receipt (LST) allows you to **trade, invest, or use it** in other ways, even while your original crypto is still staked

### How Does It Work?

1. **Staking**:
   - When you stake your cryptocurrency, like SOL or ETH, it gets locked
   - The staking provider gives you an LST (like **hSOL** or **stETH**) as proof of your stake

2. **Liquidity**:
   - Unlike traditional staking where your crypto is locked, LSTs let you keep liquidity
   - You can trade or use the LST in DeFi apps (like lending or borrowing) while still earning rewards on the original staked crypto

3. **Earning Rewards**:
   - While you hold LSTs, your rewards from staking are automatically reflected in the LST's value
   - Over time, the value of your LST grows as it includes both your original stake and the rewards

### Real-Life Example: Coffee Shop Gift Card

Imagine you go to a coffee shop and buy a **$100 gift card**:

1. **Gift Card = LST**:
   - The gift card represents your $100 (staked crypto)
   - But instead of just holding it, the coffee shop allows you to **use the card anywhere**, like trading it or paying for other services

2. **Earning Rewards**:
   - The shop rewards you for holding the gift card by adding more money to it over time
   - So after a month, the gift card might now be worth **$105** (this is like the staking rewards being reflected in the LST's value)

### Why is LST Useful?

| | Regular Staking | LST (Liquid Staking) |
|---|---|---|
| Crypto locked? | Yes — can't use it | No — get a tradeable LST receipt |
| Earn rewards? | Yes | Yes |
| Use in DeFi? | No | Yes — trade, lend, borrow the LST |
| Liquidity? | None until unstaked | Full liquidity via the LST token |

> **Simple Takeaway**: LST = Your crypto stays staked, but you get a token (receipt) to keep using it. It's like earning interest on your money in the bank while still being able to use it for investments or spending.

## 5. Assignment — Creating a Centralized LST

### The Goal

Write code that:
- **Tracks the blockchain** for incoming transactions to an address we own
- **Anytime SOL comes** to the address, we mint our custom token to that address. The amount depends on a mathematical formula
- **Whenever the user returns** some Custom Token, we return them SOL in a similar amount

> We are not actually staking the token so in the long term we will lose money. We should figure out a way to generate yield on top of user assets.

## 6. Indexer

An **Indexer** is a tool or system that **scans every block and transaction** on the blockchain and lets you **subscribe to specific events** that you are interested in.

### How Does It Work?

- The indexer constantly monitors and processes blockchain activity, looking at transactions and blocks
- It identifies and logs specific **events** happening on the blockchain

### Examples of Events It Can Track

1. **Function Calls**: A specific function is called in a smart contract or program (e.g., a swap on Raydium)
2. **Token Transfers**: SOL is transferred from one wallet to another
3. **Custom Token Events**: USDT, USDC, or a custom token is transferred to an address

### Real-Life Example

Think of an indexer like a **news alert system**:
- It watches everything happening in a city (the blockchain)
- You can set up alerts for specific events, such as:
  - "Notify me if there's a car accident at this intersection" (e.g., a function call)
  - "Notify me if $100 is deposited into my account" (e.g., a SOL transfer)
  - "Notify me if a package (custom token) is delivered to my address"

### Popular Indexers / Webhook Services

| Service | Chain | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| Helius | Solana | Webhooks for token transfers, program calls |
| The Graph | Ethereum / EVM | GraphQL queries over indexed contract events |
| Moralis | Multi-chain | REST API for token/NFT/transaction events |
| QuickNode Streams | Multi-chain | Real-time blockchain data streams |

## Summary

In today's lecture, we explored **Liquid Staking Tokens (LSTs)** in-depth:
- **Staking** = locking crypto to secure the network and earn rewards (like a fixed deposit)
- **Validators** = service providers who run infrastructure, earning commissions (not owning the staked tokens)
- **Helius** = the biggest Solana validator, unique because it charges 0% commission
- **LST exchange rate** = 1 SOL ≠ 1 HSOL because HSOL already has accumulated rewards baked in
- **LST** = receipt token for staked crypto — stays liquid, tradeable, usable in DeFi
- **Centralized LST** = our own mint/burn server: SOL in → mint custom token; custom token in → burn + send SOL back
- **Indexer** = blockchain event monitor — like a news alert system for the blockchain
